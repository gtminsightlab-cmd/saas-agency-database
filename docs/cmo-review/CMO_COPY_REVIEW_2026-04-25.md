## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Seven16 Group sits in a category that hasn't been named yet. ZoomInfo, Apollo, and D&B sell *contact data* — names, phones, titles. AM Best and S&P Global sell *carrier financials*. SNL and ALIRT sell *insurer ratings*. None of them sell **the proprietary mapping between an agent's carrier appointments and the underlying parent-group writing-company hierarchy** — and that mapping is the single highest-signal predictor of which agencies will bind a given line of business *today*.

That gap is the company. Everything else — the four verticals, the credit pricing, the dual-agent verification — is a delivery mechanism for one strategic idea: **carrier appointments are a leak in the competitor's intelligence, and we read that leak in real time.**

The current copy on the marketing site treats Seven16 like a directory product ("Unlock Agency Connections. Drive Growth."). It should treat Seven16 like a **competitive intelligence layer** sold to the people who win or lose their year on agent-of-record battles. The rewrite below moves from feature language ("36,000 agencies, 87,000 contacts") to category language ("the only system that maps the writing-company paper trail your competitor leaves behind").

**The three highest-leverage changes, in order:**

1. **Homepage hero — replace the generic value statement with a category-defining claim.** The current "Unlock Agency Connections. Drive Growth." reads like an Apollo competitor. The rewrite reads like a Bain report.
2. **Pricing narrative — move from "credits" to "appointments verified."** Buyers don't pay for downloads, they pay to land the next 5 agent-of-record wins. Reframe the unit economics.
3. **Sign-up flow — separate the "browse" persona from the "buyer" persona.** Free tier is for analysts and BD reps building lists. Paid tier is for VPs of distribution and CMOs solving an attainment problem. Different copy, different proof, different next step.

The full diagnosis, strategic frame, and page-by-page rewrites follow.

## 1 — DIAGNOSIS: WHAT THE CURRENT COPY GETS RIGHT (AND WRONG)

### What's working

The verticals page is the strongest asset on the site today. It does three things consultancies would approve of:

1. **Establishes a defensible distinction** with the parent-child carrier tree (W. R. Berkley → Carolina Casualty, AIG → Lexington). This is the proof point — readers can verify the mapping themselves.
2. **Quantifies the moat** with the tier model (Exposure / Growing / Specialist). Agencies are scored on observable behavior, not self-reported tags.
3. **Makes the cost of inaction tangible** — every month an MGU isn't using this, a competitor with the data is calling agencies that have an opening.

The homepage gets one thing right: the hygiene framing ("Distribution Intelligence decays by 3% every month"). That's a McKinsey-grade insight delivered as a tagline. **Keep it. Build the entire pricing story around it.**

### What's not working

**Problem 1 — Category drift.** The homepage hero says "Built for MGUs, wholesalers, carriers, and industry partners." Buyers in those four roles have radically different jobs-to-be-done. A wholesaler's CRO is fighting submission-to-bind ratios. A carrier's distribution VP is fighting agency consolidation. A reinsurance broker is fighting placement market access. *One headline cannot speak to all four.* Either narrow to one ICP per page, or use a vertical/role-aware hero that swaps based on referrer/UTM.

**Problem 2 — Feature parade.** The "From Intelligence to Distribution. Three clicks." section is structured like a product tour — Build / Review / Download. The reader doesn't yet know *why* they'd want to do those three things. Move the strategic claim above the product tour.

**Problem 3 — Pricing language is operational, not strategic.** The current pricing copy says "Distribution Intelligence decays at 3% per month. Keep your pipeline alive with real-time Hygiene updates." That's the right concept buried under a transactional offer. Reframe pricing as a **risk tier**: how much intelligence freshness is your distribution organization willing to forgo?

**Problem 4 — No competitive proof.** The site never names a competitor. ZoomInfo, Apollo, D&B Hoovers, and AM Best all charge 10× more for less precision in this niche. The buyer is comparing — silently. Make the comparison explicit.

**Problem 5 — Sign-up has no segmentation.** Every visitor lands on the same form. A trial user from a wholesaler should see proof points about wholesaler attainment. A user clicking from /verticals/transportation should see trucking-specific proof. **Segment at sign-up.**

## 2 — STRATEGIC POSITIONING FRAME

This is the frame the rewrite executes against. Anchor every piece of copy to one of these four pillars (Lafley/Martin's *Where to Play / How to Win* applied to a category-creating B2B platform).

### Where to Play

**The buyer is not "MGUs, wholesalers, carriers, industry partners."** That's an audience. The buyer is one of three roles, ranked by willingness-to-pay:

| Tier | Role | Job-To-Be-Done | Annual Budget for This | Decision Speed |
|------|------|---------------|------------------------|----------------|
| **A** | VP / Director of Distribution at a wholesaler or MGU | "Hit our agent-of-record growth number this quarter" | $25K–$100K | 30 days |
| **B** | Head of Marketing / Demand at a carrier program team | "Source 50 new appointed agencies in our specialty by Q4" | $15K–$50K | 60 days |
| **C** | Founder / BD lead at a startup wholesaler or MGA | "Build distribution from zero without a $100K AM Best subscription" | $5K–$15K | 7 days |

**Tier A converts at the highest LTV.** Every word on the homepage should be written for Tier A first. Tier C is where free-trial signups come from but isn't where revenue concentrates.

### How to Win

The defensible positioning is one sentence:

> **Seven16 is the only platform that maps the writing-company paper trail behind every U.S. commercial agency, refreshed monthly against state filings — turning the appointment list into a verified inventory of which agents can bind your program today.**

That sentence does five things competitors can't:

1. **Names a category** ("writing-company paper trail") that buyers recognize but no one else owns.
2. **Establishes a moat** ("mapped to parent-group hierarchy" — proprietary knowledge graph).
3. **Anchors freshness** ("refreshed monthly against state filings" — operational discipline).
4. **Defines the use case in their language** ("which agents can bind your program today" — buyer-side language, not seller-side).
5. **Implies a comparison** without naming names. ZoomInfo doesn't have appointments. Apollo doesn't have parent-group mapping. AM Best has financials, not behavior.

### Capabilities (what to actually sell)

Three layers, sold as one platform but valued separately by different buyers:

1. **Knowledge graph** — the parent-child carrier mapping (400+ writing companies → 60+ parent groups). This is the IP. Tier A buyers value this most.
2. **Vertical inference engine** — appointment-based specialization scoring (Exposure / Growing / Specialist). Tier B buyers value this most.
3. **Hygiene cadence** — the monthly refresh + dual-agent verification. Tier A and B both value this; it's the reason they renew.

### Management Systems (what makes the buyer trust you'll deliver)

Three operational facts to surface in copy without bragging:

- **Dual-Agent Verification Pipeline** — every record reviewed by two independent agents before publishing. (This is the "trust signal" that justifies the premium over ZoomInfo.)
- **Monthly state-filings sync** — every carrier roster cross-checked against state DOI public filings. (This is what makes "refreshed monthly" non-trivial — it's not just a re-scrape.)
- **Auditable provenance** — every appointment row carries a "verified-as-of" date the buyer can see in the UI. (This is the credibility layer.)

## 3 — VOICE & TONE GUIDE

The voice is **what a McKinsey insurance practice partner would write if they were also the founder.** Specifically:

- **Authoritative without arrogance.** State facts, don't argue. "Carolina Casualty is a W.R. Berkley writing company" — not "we believe Carolina Casualty rolls up to W.R. Berkley."
- **Operator's language, not vendor's language.** "Submission-to-bind ratio." "Agent of record." "Specialty paper." "Roster cross-check." Use the words the Tier A buyer says in their Monday meeting.
- **Quantified everywhere.** Never write "many agencies" — write "5,189 agricultural agencies." Never write "fast updates" — write "refreshed every 30 days against state filings."
- **No marketing hype words.** Banned: *unleash, supercharge, revolutionary, game-changing, AI-powered, next-gen.* The word **AI** appears once in the entire site, and only inside a specific feature name ("Dual-Agent Verification Pipeline").
- **Active voice, second person.** "You'll see which agencies hold Acadia paper" — not "agencies holding Acadia paper will be visible."
- **Short sentences. One idea per sentence.** McKinsey reports look intimidating because of the slides, not the prose. The prose is short.
- **No exclamation points anywhere on the marketing site.** None.

When in doubt, ask: *would a Tier-A buyer print this and bring it to a Monday distribution meeting?* If no, rewrite.
